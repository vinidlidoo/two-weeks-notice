# vincent-voice

Writing rules collected from feedback across sessions. Apply when producing any prose on Vincent's behalf.

## Register

- **First person, informal.** Writing to a collaborator (Pavan), not to a board. Keep "I think," "I'd," "my worry is." Do not drift into corporate or pitch voice.
- **Constructive without watering down.** Doubt and disagreement stay, just phrased as a thought, not a dismissal. "This feels off to me because X" beats "I don't know about this."
- **Technical depth is welcome.** Vincent has real Ethereum/ML/evals background. Don't simplify defensively. But no jargon for jargon's sake.

## Punctuation & phrasing

- **Em-dashes used sparingly.** Vincent's explicit feedback: they get used way too often. Prefer commas, colons, periods, or parenthetical rephrasings. Em-dashes inside proper names (e.g., "Rubric — Self-Improvement Loops") are fine — it's the connector-dash in prose that's the issue. Budget: at most one em-dash per paragraph, ideally zero.
- **Specific beats vague.** Reject phrases like "worth connecting the dots," "food for thought," "let's circle back." Say what the actual implication or next step is.
- **No hyperbolic qualifiers.** Reject "single most important," "the real thesis," "the scariest," "the biggest risk," "fundamentally." Replace with the specific thing, or cut. Vincent's feedback should read as measured, not dramatic.
- **No cheesy collab-speak.** Avoid "good sign of alignment," "on the same page," "let's align," "win-win." Vincent and his collaborator aren't trying to "align"; they're trying to find what excites both of them.
- **No pitch voice.** Lines like "whoever owns X owns Y," "the prize is…", "this becomes the default," "mandatory infrastructure" — cut them. Not writing for investors.
- **Precise word choice.** "Clearest thread" is wrong when he means "most exciting thread." Match the word to the actual sentiment (excitement, doubt, curiosity), not a generic adjective.

## Structure

- **Length matches task.** Short comments: 1–3 sentences. Idea summaries: 2–4 sentences, ~350–500 chars. End-of-response summaries: 1–2 sentences. Don't pad.
- **Lead with substance; punch the opener.** Skip "I wanted to share some thoughts…" preambles. The first sentence carries the claim, doesn't build up to it. If the opener could be cut without losing the point, cut it.
- **Unpack the stakes.** When naming a problem, a risk, or a pressure, explain *why* it matters before moving on. Don't leave the reader to infer the implication.
- **Questions stay questions.** If Vincent is asking, the output asks.
- **No trailing "hope this helps" / meta-commentary.**

## When editing his input (dictation, drafts)

- **Preserve every distinct point, question, concern, reference** (names, numbers, examples) Vincent made. Do not add new arguments, examples, or context.
- **Cut filler ruthlessly.** Remove "um," "uh," "you know," "like," restarts, repetition. Collapse rambles into single clear sentences.
- **Don't sharpen beyond his intent.** Never escalate criticism to something harsher than what he said, never add praise he didn't give.

## Known-rejected phrases (anti-examples)

- "good sign of alignment"
- "worth connecting the dots"
- "worth naming before you see the numbers"
- "whoever owns X owns the layer above them"
- "single most important research item"
- "earn the right to expand"
- "plug into a deployed agent stack"
- "the real thesis is…"
- "the real work is…" (cousin of "the real thesis")
- "the brief's payoff" (pitch voice — a brief-to-self doesn't have a payoff)
